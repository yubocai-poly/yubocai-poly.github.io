HSS151 FINAL PAPER

A glimpse into the development of French science through the equipment of the Lavoisier experiment

Yubo Cai
Apr 17, 2022 History Topics
Image credit: Unsplash

1. Introduction

The Musée des Arts et Métiers was founded in 1794 on the initiative of the Grégoire clergy with the purpose of “preserving new and useful inventions”. Nowadays, it is a museum of continuous technological innovation. This monumental museum displays the continuous development of mankind in industrial technology, recording every moment of technological innovation.

The most profound impact on me is still the apparatus and equipment of the Lavoisier experiment, an important experiment that started the development of modern human science. More importantly, we are able to see in Lavoisier’s life experience the process of scientific development in France as a whole, and some of the experience still remain even today, profoundly influencing generations of Polytechniciens.

2. Maintext

Enter the Musée des Arts et Métiers, pass through the hall of Arago and Poincaré, then you will arrive at Le labotatoire de Lavoisier.This is where Lavoisier completed his experiments on the determination of the oxygen content in the air, and it can even be said that this is where chemistry was born. With this experiment, Lavoisier overturned the Phlogiston theory, which had been considered correct for nearly a century, and developed the methodology for the research of modern chemistry.

In 1743, Lavoisier was born in Paris, France, to a noble family of lawyers. From an early age, he demonstrated extraordinary love of natural science. However, his family arranged for Lavoisier to attend law school at the University of Paris, and he continued to study natural sciences in his free time. After graduating from university, Lavoisier remained in France on geological expeditions, which led to the creation of the first map of France. At the age of 25, Lavoisier became a member of the Académie des sciences. Then he began to prepare the world-changing oxygen content measurement experiments, also known as the Lavoisier experiment.

For this experiment, Ravasi spent a lot of human and financial resources to build his own laboratory. For example, high-precision scales, and special sealed combustion vessels made of copper. Considering the toxic gases produced by the burning of mercury through intense heat, Lavoisier made his own early gas mask and heat-insulating gloves for this reason. At the same time, Lavoisier also designed the most important instrument in this experiment - the retort. However, due to the limitations of the glass manufacturing process at that time, the retort manufacturing process was extremely complicated, so Lavoisier found the most exquisite French glass artisans to customize the retort for experimental use.

In order to prove his suspicions, Lavoisier put a small amount of mercury in a closed container and heated it continuously for twelve days, and found that part of the silver-white liquid mercury turned into red powder, while the volume of air in the container was reduced by almost one-fifth. Lavoisier studied the remaining portion of air and found that it could not support the combustion of combustible materials, mistaking the gas for nitrogen (Latin for “not life-sustaining”). Lavoisier then collected the red powder generated on the surface of the mercury (now proved to be mercury oxide) and placed it in another smaller container after intense heat to obtain mercury and oxygen, and the volume of oxygen was exactly equal to the volume of air reduced in the original closed container.

With his extraordinary research, Lavoisier proposed the nomenclature of modern chemistry in the Méthode de nomenclature chimique, and then, using the law of conservation of mass based on oxidation, Lavoisier published the Traité Élémentaire de Chimie in 1789, which became the first true modern chemistry textbook. In addition, he helped to establish the metric system and became the true founder of the discipline of chemistry.

Lavoisier was arrested in 1794, convicted on May 8, and guillotined on the same day along with 27 other tax collectors. A great man, Lavoisier, died in the midst of political storm, The end of his brilliant but unfortunate life.

3. Impact on future generations

In 1789, Napoleon launched the coup d’état du 18 Brumaire to take over all affairs in France, starting a 15-year period of military dictatorship. Influenced by Lavoisier, Napoleon attached great importance to the development of education, thus reorganizing the Ecole Polytechnique and reopening the Ecole Normale Supérieure in Paris during the Empire, two universities that had a profound impact on the development of modern France. Due to the end of the French Revolution, Joseph Lagrange, Joseph Fourier and others had a stable research environment, thus promoting the rapid development of mathematics, physics and natural sciences in France.
